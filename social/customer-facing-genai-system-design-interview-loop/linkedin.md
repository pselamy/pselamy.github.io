Customer-facing GenAI system design is becoming its own interview loop.

Classic system design prep is still useful, but it does not cover the whole shape of these roles. The conversation often starts with an ambiguous customer problem:

"A customer wants an AI assistant over internal documents. How would you start?"

A strong answer is not just "use RAG."

You have to discover requirements, protect customer data, design retrieval, decide whether tools or agents are warranted, evaluate groundedness, monitor latency and cost, troubleshoot bad answers, and translate field pain into product feedback.

That is a different skill set from memorizing a feed architecture or a cache pattern.

I could not find preparation material that treated this loop with enough depth, so I built a public course around it:

- discovery and requirements
- enterprise RAG architecture
- vector search and retrieval modeling
- agents, tool calling, workflows, and state
- IAM, PII, tenant isolation, and audit
- evaluation, groundedness, and hallucination controls
- observability, latency, cost, and reliability
- troubleshooting slow, inaccurate, or expensive systems
- case studies and timed mocks

The course is built as a NotebookLM series with a public table of contents, study paths, quizzes, flashcards, mind maps, mock drills, rubrics, and final review material.

The broader lesson: when preparation material does not exist for a role shape that is clearly emerging, you can build the curriculum yourself.

Not as a prompt dump. Not as AI slop. As a structured learning system: source material, active recall, mocks, evaluation rubrics, and a public artifact that can help the next person too.
